Case Study

Mayo Clinic Radiology Wheelchair Project

A human-centered medical device design collaboration with Mayo Clinic Phoenix Radiology to reduce chest X-ray fall risk while protecting image quality.

EPICS

The project started with a clear clinical problem: some patients coming in for chest X-rays were weak, dizzy, post-surgery, medicated, or reliant on assistive devices, yet they were often asked to stand for imaging.

That created a fall risk, but the safer alternative of imaging patients while lying down could reduce diagnostic quality. Our team worked with Mayo Clinic stakeholders to translate that workflow pain point into engineering requirements for a safer seating-based imaging solution.

The result was a modified Mayo-approved wheelchair concept designed to stay familiar to staff, fit hospital constraints, and preserve upright patient support without creating visible X-ray artifacts.

We explored concepts such as radiolucent materials, rotating armrests, reclining backs, and joint designs that could move obstructive components out of the imaging field.

Over time, the design converged toward a reclining-back wheelchair with arms that could rotate farther away from the X-ray field, while still remaining transportable, disinfectant-resistant, and practical for one technologist to use.

The prototype and documentation process included stakeholder interviews, needs assessment, CAD modeling, prototyping, validation planning, and testing ideas for transportability, cleaning resistance, load safety, and image visibility.

A major insight from the project was that even small wheelchair artifacts can mimic clinical findings on scans, so every design choice had to balance patient safety with image quality.

Future Forward Award

The team delivered the final prototype to Mayo Clinic in May 2024 and received the Future Forward Award from EPICS for successfully completing the project.

We also prepared pitch materials, placed first in the EPICS Elite Pitch Competition with $2,500 in funding, and later placed second in the Ashton Family Venture Challenge with $1,500.

For a quick walkthrough, think of the project as a translation of clinical workflow pain points into engineering requirements, then into CAD, prototype, and validation planning.
