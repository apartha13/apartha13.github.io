Case Study

Honors Thesis: AI and Ethics in Filmmaking

A qualitative thesis exploring how AI can support filmmaking while preserving authorship, consent, and creative integrity.

Research

Abstract

This thesis investigates a central tension in contemporary filmmaking: while artificial intelligence can improve efficiency across creative workflows, its adoption introduces unresolved ethical concerns around authorship, consent, labor displacement, and public trust.

Using qualitative methods, the study combines literature review with visual media case analysis to evaluate both the opportunities and governance risks of AI-assisted production.

Findings show that AI can productively support ideation, planning, and post-production tasks, but only when deployed within frameworks that preserve human creative authority and require explicit safeguards for consent and transparency.

The thesis argues for a complementarity model, in which AI augments human storytelling rather than replacing it, and proposes recommendations centered on consent-first design, disclosure standards, and accountable policy practice.

Research Design

Questions and methodological approach

Research Questions

  • How does AI reshape authorship across pre-production, production, and post-production?
  • What forms of consent are required when synthetic media uses human likeness or voice?
  • Where do efficiency gains conflict with creative labor protections?
  • What governance mechanisms improve transparency and reduce misuse?

Methodology

  • Qualitative literature review across ethics, media studies, and AI policy.
  • Case-based analysis of AI use in film-adjacent production contexts.
  • Comparative coding across authenticity, labor, authorship, and consent themes.
  • Synthesis into normative recommendations for responsible adoption.

Primary Evidence

Key findings and thesis opening page

Key Findings

  • AI tools can reduce iteration time in script and edit workflows without removing human direction.
  • Authorship becomes ambiguous when models contribute significant generative content.
  • Consent frameworks are often underdeveloped for synthetic voice and likeness generation.
  • Disclosure and provenance mechanisms are necessary to protect audience trust.

Presentation

Thesis defense

The defense emphasized a practical research contribution: a structured way to evaluate where AI supports film production and where ethical controls must be explicitly enforced.

Discussion focused on implementation-level guidance for creators and institutions, including consent protocols, transparency disclosure, and preservation of human creative authority.
